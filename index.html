About

I am a PhD candidate in Computer Science at the University of Maryland, College Park, advised by Dr. Zhicheng Liu in the Human-Data Interaction Group.

My dissertation, titled "Beyond Domain Boundaries: Enhancing Generalizability of Event Sequence Visual Analytics Research," focuses on developing domain-agnostic frameworks for sequential data modeling and visualization.

My research interests span event sequence analysis, data visualization, causal inference, and vision-language models. I have developed novel benchmarks for evaluating VLMs on process visualizations and created frameworks for detecting causal relationships in temporal data under distribution shifts.

I have published at top-tier venues including IEEE VIS, EuroVis, CHI, and EMNLP, and have research experience at Amazon Web Services developing frameworks for causal relationship discovery in temporal data.

Research

ProcVQA: Benchmarking Vision-Language Models on Process Visualizations

Created the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating Vision-Language Models (VLMs) on process visualizations, featuring 118 charts across three visualization types and eight domains. Developed dual-task evaluation protocol with 144 expert-authored questions and 2,583 ground truth annotations. Evaluated 19 state-of-the-art VLMs, revealing critical performance degradation patterns and failure modes.

Vision-Language Models Benchmark Evaluation

Causal Relation Shifts in Event Sequences

Developed a novel causal framework extending Rubin's causal model to temporal point processes for detecting causal relationship changes under distribution shifts. Engineered a hybrid Transformer-CNN architecture achieving 88% reduction in RMSE and 90% in MAE on industrial maintenance data. Research conducted during internships at Amazon Web Services.

Causal Inference Deep Learning Time Series

Multi-Level Task Framework for Event Sequence Analysis

Developed a comprehensive domain-agnostic task framework through systematic analysis of 58 visualization systems across 16 academic venues. Created a four-level hierarchical taxonomy capturing objectives, intents, strategies, and techniques. Established theoretical foundation for next-generation intelligent assistance tools in event sequence analytics.

Taxonomy Framework Event Sequences

Visual Summarization Techniques Evaluation

Conducted large-scale empirical evaluation with 180 participants across 108 unique visual summaries and 6 datasets. Reimplemented three visualization techniques with standardized visual encoding. Established the first benchmarking protocol for visual summarization techniques, identifying content and interpretability as key quality factors.

Human Study Visualization Evaluation
